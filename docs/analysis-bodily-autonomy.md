# Bodily Autonomy and Abortion — A Logical Consistency Analysis

An epistemological analysis of a pro-choice position derived from classical liberal first principles, stress-tested against the Logic System's framework.

---

## The Position

**Axiom:** Humans have unalienable right to bodily autonomy.

**Premise:** A fetus qualifies as a human with full rights almost immediately — certainly by the zygote stage.

**Conclusion:** Abortion is permissible before viability because the mother's bodily autonomy supersedes — not because the fetus lacks rights, but because the fetus cannot exercise its rights independently of her body. After viability, abortion should not be permitted because the dependency on *her body specifically* has ended.

---

## The Logical Structure

### The Key Distinction: Body vs Resources

Bodily autonomy is the right to what happens *inside your body*, not the right to withhold resources that exist *outside your body*.

| Situation | What's Required | From Whom Specifically | Bodily Autonomy Applies? |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|-------------------------|
| Pre-viability pregnancy | The mother's body as biological life support | Only the mother — no one else can provide this | **Yes** — compelling continuation = compelling surrender of her body |
| Post-viability pregnancy | Sustenance and care in general | Any qualified caregiver | **No** — dependency is no longer on her body specifically |
| Post-birth child | Food, shelter, care | Any caregiver; obligation is transferable (adoption, foster care) | **No** — failing to provide external resources is negligence, not an exercise of autonomy |

### Why the Fetus's Rights Don't Override

Both the mother and the fetus have full unalienable rights. The question is purely mechanical: **can the fetus exercise its rights independently of the mother's body?**

- If **no** (pre-viability): the mother's autonomy governs. You cannot compel one person to be another person's biological life support system. Society does not compel blood donation, organ donation, or bone marrow donation — even to save a life. Pregnancy, under this logic, is the same category.
- If **yes** (post-viability): the fetus's right to life governs. The dependency on *her specifically* has ended. The fetus can be delivered and cared for by others. Termination at this point is no longer an autonomy claim — it's the termination of a viable human who no longer requires her body.

### Why Post-Birth Negligence Is Not a Bodily Autonomy Claim

Starving a born child is not an exercise of bodily autonomy because no one is asking the mother to use *her body*. They're asking her to provide external resources — food, shelter, care. She can transfer that obligation entirely (adoption, foster care, family). She cannot transfer a pregnancy. The distinction between "providing your literal body as life support" and "providing resources that anyone could provide" is the load-bearing difference.

## The Technology Clause

The position is explicitly **technology-dependent**: the viability threshold changes as medical technology advances.

- In 1973 (Roe v. Wade): viability was ~28 weeks.
- In 2024: viability is ~22-24 weeks.
- Future: if artificial womb technology makes viability possible at 12 weeks, the permissible window for abortion shrinks to 12 weeks under this framework.

This means the position **updates with evidence** rather than being fixed to a moral line. The Logic System classifies this as epistemologically superior to:

- **"Life begins at conception, therefore no abortion ever"** — ignores the bodily autonomy question entirely. Does not engage with the competing-rights problem.
- **"My body my choice, no restrictions until birth"** — ignores viability. Does not engage with the fact that a viable fetus can survive independently.

Both of those positions are fixed and unfalsifiable. This position is dynamic and evidence-responsive.

## Epistemological Classification

Using the Logic System's taxonomy:

| Component of the Argument | Epistemological Status |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| "Humans have unalienable right to bodily autonomy" | **Axiom (value premise)** — not provable, but stated as a foundational commitment |
| "A fetus is a human from conception" | **Verifiable observation** — biological fact, conceding the strongest pro-life premise |
| "No person can be compelled to provide their body as life support for another" | **Logical derivation from the axiom** — follows necessarily if the axiom is accepted |
| "Viability is the threshold because it's when body-dependency ends" | **Model-dependent but empirically grounded** — the threshold is defined by medical capability, not by ideology |
| "Post-birth negligence is different from bodily autonomy" | **Logical distinction** — providing external resources ≠ providing your body. The obligation is transferable; pregnancy is not. |

**No category errors detected.** The position does not present value judgments as observations, does not present tacit consensus as known truth, and does not present unfalsifiable claims as empirical findings. Each component is correctly identified as what it is (axiom, observation, derivation, or model).
