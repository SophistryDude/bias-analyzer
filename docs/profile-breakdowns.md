#### Notes

The most significant data point is the collapse of ideological coherence. Owens started as a coherent conservative voice — her early positions were internally consistent and predictable. #### Notes

**CRITICAL — PERSONA DIVERGENCE:** Fuentes maintains two distinct presentation modes. His public-facing content is moderated and measured — you may not agree, but you can follow the reasoning. His personal streaming platform content is significantly more extreme on every axis. The scores above represent a blended assessment. The private persona would push the progressive and liberal/conservative axes past the scale.

This divergence is itself a significant data point. It suggests strategic positioning relative to the Overton window — Fuentes calibrates his public positions to sit just inside (or just outside) the boundary of acceptable discourse, while his actual positions are further out. His liberal/conservative score of +0.90 actually exceeds our definition of "conservative" (preserve status quo of previous 1-2 generations). He wants to roll back changes from multiple generations, which is closer to reactionary than conservative. Our model may need a sixth degree beyond conservative for this.

His free-speech advocacy is primarily instrumental — he advocates free speech as protection for his own expression rather than as a principled position defending speech he disagrees with.

#### Notes

The most ideologically coherent profile in our dataset. Shapiro's positions are internally consistent and predictable — if you know his framework (religious conservative, free-market, anti-progressive, interventionist), you can predict his take on a new issue with high accuracy. Foreign policy is his biggest divergence from the populist right. His interventionist stance (particularly regarding Israel and Middle East policy) puts him at odds with the isolationist trend in right-wing media (Carlson, Fuentes, etc.).

## Axis Definitions

These axes are **independent** — a person's position on one axis does not determine their position on another.

### Economic System: Capitalist (-1) ←→ Communist (+1)

How should economic resources be owned and allocated? Private markets vs. collective/state control. Most people fall somewhere in between (mixed economy, regulated capitalism, social democracy).

### Speech: Free Speech Absolutist (-1) ←→ Reasonable Censorship (+1)

Should institutions restrict expression to prevent harm, or is unrestricted expression the safeguard? This axis has flipped its partisan alignment in the last decade — the left used to be the free-speech side (ACLU), the right used to favor censorship (obscenity laws). Now it's reversed.

### Progressive: Progressive/Equity (-1) ←→ Non-Progressive/Efficiency (+1)

**This is NOT the same as liberal/conservative.** Rooted in the Progressive Era (1890s-1920s). Progressives believe society should provide targeted equity — differential support calibrated to individual disadvantage — to produce more equal outcomes. Non-progressives argue that equity interventions introduce friction and perverse incentives that reduce overall system output (the Pareto principle: the unequal distribution is a natural emergent property of complex systems; flattening it degrades the whole rather than uplifting the bottom).

### Liberal/Conservative: Liberal (-1) ←→ Conservative (+1)

**This is about pace and risk tolerance for change, not direction.** Liberal: willing to change the status quo because equality and freedom are worth the risk. Conservative: the status quo of the previous 1-2 generations represents accumulated wisdom; the burden of proof is on those proposing change.

Key distinction: A liberal might want change through deregulation (change, but non-progressive). A progressive might want existing equity programs preserved (which is conservative about those programs).

### Foreign Policy: Isolationist (-1) ←→ Interventionist/War Hawk (+1)

Should the nation focus domestically and avoid military entanglements, or actively project power internationally? This axis cuts across all others — there are isolationists and hawks on both the left and the right.

## Coherence & Driver Types

Each profile is assessed for whether positions appear to be:

- **Philosophy-driven:** Positions are internally consistent, stable over time, and predictable from first principles. The person has a genuine ideological framework. (Example: Shapiro, coherence 0.80)
- **Engagement-driven:** Positions shift frequently, show low internal consistency, and appear to align with whatever generates audience attention. (Example: Owens, coherence 0.40)
- **Mixed:** Some axes are stable while others are in flux. May represent genuine evolution or partial audience capture. (Example: DeFranco, coherence 0.60)

Coherence doesn't mean correctness. A person can be consistently wrong. But it does tell you whether you're dealing with someone who has a framework (argue the framework) or someone who is performing (track the incentives).
